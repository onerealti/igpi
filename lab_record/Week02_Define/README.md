# Week 02: Define Phase – POV & Problem Statement

## Aim
To identify user-centered problems and convert them into clear, actionable opportunity statements.

## Objectives
*   Understand user needs based on empathy interviews.
*   Identify primary mechanical and electrical pain points in solar tracking.
*   Develop Point-of-View (POV) statements for different target personas.
*   Reframe engineering challenges into design opportunities using "How Might We" (HMW) statements.

## Theory
### Point-of-View (POV) Statements
A POV statement is a meaningful and actionable problem statement that focuses on the user, their needs, and the insights gathered during the Empathize stage. Framing the problem this way keeps the design human-centered.
*   **Format:** `[User] needs a way to [user's need] because [insight].`

### Reframing and "How Might We" (HMW) Statements
HMW statements are short questions that launch brainstorming. They reframe the constraints of a POV into open-ended questions that spark creative solutions.

---

## Activity

### 1. User Observations & Need Identification
Based on field research, we identified key limitations:
*   Fixed arrays are highly inefficient in changing seasons.
*   Users fear mechanical breakdown and power loss from tracking motors.
*   Manual intervention to reset or calibrate trackers is undesirable.

### 2. Five Point-of-View (POV) Statements
We drafted POVs for five distinct user segments:

1.  **The Remote Cabin Owner** needs a way to maximize solar power during low-light winter months because their current fixed-mount system fails to keep batteries charged for essential medical equipment.
2.  **The Urban Sustainability Advocate** needs an aesthetically pleasing and compact tracking system because they have limited balcony space and want to prove that small-scale solar is viable in cities.
3.  **The DIY Off-Gridder** needs a tracker built from modular, 3D-printable parts because they live far from specialized repair shops and need to easily maintain the mechanism themselves.
4.  **The Agricultural IoT Developer** needs a low-power solar tracker for remote field sensors because manual battery changes in the field are expensive and time-consuming.
5.  **The Education Tech Instructor** needs a transparent, visible mechanism because they want students to clearly visualize the relationship between light intensity (LDR values) and motor movement (Azimuth/Elevation).

### 3. Final "How Might We" (HMW) Statement
To address our target user group (residential off-grid users and cabin owners), we formulated our final design challenge:

> **How might we design a robust, low-cost dual-axis solar tracking system that maximizes energy harvest for off-grid residential users while ensuring the mechanism remains energy-efficient and easy to maintain?**

### 4. Design Goals & Specifications
To evaluate solutions, we established three primary design targets:
*   **Accuracy:** Maintain the solar panel within $\pm 5$ degrees of the perpendicular light rays.
*   **Reliability:** Achieve a minimum of 1,000 continuous cycles without mechanical or structural failure.
*   **Efficiency:** Implement software-based sleep modes to ensure idle tracker consumption is kept below 5% of the panel's power generation.

---

## Deliverables
1.  **User Observations Log** and problem analysis.
2.  **Five POV Statements** covering diverse user personas.
3.  **Reframed Design Challenge (HMW Statement)**.
4.  **Design Goals & Target Specifications**.

## Outcome
Students learn how to define clear, actionable, and user-centered engineering problems, transitioning from broad empathy feedback into specific design requirements.
